# Does ginger help blood sugar or body weight?

Ginger shows up constantly in "foods for blood sugar" and "foods for weight loss" lists, usually
backed by a vague nod to its gingerol compounds. Two separate 2024 meta-analyses actually tested
these claims with pooled trial data — one on glycemic control, one on body weight and composition.
The results don't agree with each other, which makes for a more honest story than either claim on
its own.

## The proposed mechanism

Ginger's pungent compounds — gingerols and shogaols — are thought to act on metabolism in a few
ways: activating AMPK (a cellular energy sensor linked to fat oxidation and insulin sensitivity),
slowing gastric emptying (which can blunt post-meal glucose spikes and increase fullness), and a
mild thermogenic effect. That's a plausible story for *both* blood sugar and weight — but a
mechanism being plausible isn't the same as trial data confirming it.

## What the trials actually found

**Blood sugar: no significant pooled effect.** A systematic review and meta-analysis of five
randomized controlled trials in people with type 2 diabetes (ginger capsules, 1.2–2 g/day for
4–12 weeks) found **no statistically significant pooled effect** on fasting blood glucose or
HbA1c. Two of the five individual trials did show a significant drop in fasting glucose on their
own, but once pooled together the effect washed out. The authors concluded the published evidence
is still too limited to confirm a glycemic benefit
([Schumacher et al., Clinical Nutrition ESPEN, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39053695/)).

**Body weight: a small but real effect.** A separate dose-response meta-analysis pooled 27
randomized controlled trials (1,309 participants) and found ginger supplementation was associated
with modest reductions in body weight (-1.52 kg), BMI (-0.58 kg/m²), waist circumference (-1.04 cm),
and body fat percentage (-0.87%) — all statistically significant. The effective dose was around
2 g/day, and trials lasting longer than 8 weeks showed a bigger effect
([Rafieipour et al., Nutrition Reviews, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38261398/)).

## The honest caveats

- The **blood-sugar claim is currently unsupported** by pooled trial data — worth knowing if that's
  the reason you were reaching for ginger.
- The **body-weight effect is real but small**, and the authors themselves rated the certainty of
  evidence as **low to very low** — meaning further research could still shift these numbers.
  A ~1.5 kg average weight change is not a substitute for diet and exercise fundamentals.
- Both analyses pooled short trials (weeks, not years) using concentrated ginger supplements, not
  necessarily the amount in a cup of ginger tea or a few slices in a stir-fry.

## The takeaway

If you're taking ginger specifically hoping it will move your blood glucose numbers, the current
evidence doesn't back that up. If your interest is body weight or waist circumference, there's a
modest, low-certainty signal worth knowing about — but not a dramatic one. See the full citation
list on the [ginger entry](/items/ginger), or browse other [foods for blood sugar & metabolism](/goals/blood-sugar).
