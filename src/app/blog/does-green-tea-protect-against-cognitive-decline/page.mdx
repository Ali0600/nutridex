# Does green tea protect against cognitive decline?

[Green tea](/items/green-tea) is already on NutriDex for the caffeine-and-L-theanine combination
that sharpens same-day focus. A meta-analysis newly published in *Neuroepidemiology* (Zhou et al.,
2026) asks a different, longer-horizon question: does habitually drinking green tea track with a
lower risk of cognitive impairment over the years, not just a calmer afternoon?

## The likely mechanism

Green tea is rich in catechins — chiefly **EGCG (epigallocatechin gallate)** — polyphenols proposed
to cross the blood-brain barrier and act as antioxidants, damping the oxidative stress and chronic
inflammation implicated in age-related cognitive decline. Green tea's L-theanine is also studied
separately for its calming effect on brain activity. None of this is proof that drinking tea rewires
long-term dementia risk — it's the biological story that would explain an association *if* one
exists, which is exactly what this meta-analysis went looking for.

## What the meta-analysis found

The review searched PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, and the Cochrane Library for observational
studies (published September 2004–September 2024) relating green tea consumption to cognitive
impairment, and pooled **18 studies covering 58,929 participants**
([Zhou, Zhu, Ren, Wu & Liu, *Neuroepidemiology*, 2026](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39947165/)).
Study quality, assessed with the Newcastle-Ottawa Scale, was rated high overall. Pooled results:

- **Green tea consumption was inversely associated with cognitive impairment overall**: odds ratio
  0.63 (95% CI 0.54–0.73).
- The association held for both **dementia** (OR 0.74, 95% CI 0.56–0.99) and **mild cognitive
  impairment** (OR 0.64, 95% CI 0.43–0.96) analyzed separately.
- It held for **both sexes** — women (OR 0.51, 95% CI 0.28–0.95) and men (OR 0.47, 95% CI 0.28–0.80).
- The **highest-consumption group** had a lower risk than low/no consumption (OR 0.64, 95% CI
  0.50–0.82), and the benefit was largest in people aged 50–69.

## The honest caveats

- **These are observational studies, not trials.** An odds ratio from cohort/case-control data shows
  an association, not proof that tea itself causes the lower risk — diet, exercise, social
  engagement, and other habits that cluster with regular tea drinking could share the credit.
- **Reverse causation is a real alternative explanation.** People in the early, undetected stages of
  cognitive decline may already be drinking less tea (changing routines, appetite, or habits) before
  a diagnosis is made, which would inflate the apparent protective effect.
- **The effect was only significant in Asian populations** — the pooled data found **no significant
  association in European populations**. The authors don't fully explain why; it could be fewer
  European studies (lower statistical power), differences in typical tea intake or brewing, or
  genuine population differences. Either way, this is not evidence that generalizes cleanly
  worldwide.
- The authors themselves call for **large-scale longitudinal studies** to confirm dose-response and
  long-term causality — this meta-analysis is a strong signal to investigate further, not a closed
  case.

## The takeaway

Pooled across 18 observational studies and nearly 59,000 people, higher green tea consumption
tracked with meaningfully lower odds of both dementia and mild cognitive impairment — but the
evidence is correlational, and the association didn't hold in European cohorts specifically. It's a
reasonable data point in favor of a daily habit you might already enjoy for
[brain, focus & mood](/goals/brain-focus), not a guarantee. Read the full evidence and citations on
the [green tea entry](/items/green-tea).
