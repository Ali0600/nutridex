# Why beetroot lowers blood pressure

Beetroot has a reputation as a blood-pressure food — and unlike a lot of nutrition folklore, this
one holds up in randomized controlled trials. The reason comes down to one molecule beetroot happens
to be unusually rich in: **inorganic nitrate**.

## The nitrate-to-nitric-oxide pathway

Beetroot is one of the most concentrated dietary sources of nitrate. Here's the chain of events
after you drink beetroot juice:

1. **Nitrate → nitrite.** Bacteria living on the surface of your tongue reduce dietary nitrate
   (NO₃⁻) to nitrite (NO₂⁻). This step literally depends on your oral microbiome — using antibacterial
   mouthwash blunts the effect.
2. **Nitrite → nitric oxide.** In the acidic, low-oxygen environment of the stomach and tissues,
   nitrite is further reduced to **nitric oxide (NO)**.
3. **Nitric oxide relaxes blood vessels.** NO signals the smooth muscle lining your arteries to
   relax, widening the vessel (vasodilation). A wider pipe means blood pushes less hard on the
   walls — and blood pressure falls.

## What the trials actually found

In a landmark 2008 study, healthy volunteers who drank about 500 ml of beetroot juice saw their
blood pressure drop within hours, tracking the rise in blood nitrite. Crucially, when a separate
group avoided swallowing their saliva (interrupting step 1), the effect disappeared — pinning the
mechanism on the nitrate pathway.

Later work extended this from "healthy volunteers for a few hours" to "people with actual
hypertension over weeks." A 2015 randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial found that daily
dietary nitrate produced **sustained** blood-pressure reductions in hypertensive patients.

## The practical takeaway

- The active ingredient is nitrate, so **other nitrate-rich foods** (spinach, arugula, celery)
  work through the same pathway.
- Don't kill the bacteria that do step 1 — heavy antibacterial mouthwash use can blunt the benefit.
- It's a real but **modest** effect and not a replacement for prescribed medication. Talk to a
  clinician about managing blood pressure.
