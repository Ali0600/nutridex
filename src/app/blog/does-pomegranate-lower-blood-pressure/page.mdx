# Does pomegranate lower blood pressure?

Pomegranate is one of the most antioxidant-dense fruits, and it has a reputation as a heart food.
On blood pressure specifically, individual trials have been a bit all over the place — so the useful
question is what happens when you pool them. Three separate meta-analyses published between 2023 and
2025 did exactly that, and they line up in the same direction.

## The likely mechanism

Pomegranate is rich in **punicalagins** — unusually large polyphenol antioxidants. The leading
explanation for a blood-pressure effect is that these polyphenols support **nitric-oxide
availability** and reduce oxidative stress in the lining of blood vessels. Nitric oxide signals the
vessel wall to relax and widen (vasodilation), and a wider vessel means blood pushes less hard
against it. It's a different starting point from beetroot's [dietary-nitrate
pathway](/blog/why-beetroot-lowers-blood-pressure), but the two converge on the same endpoint:
relaxed, more responsive arteries.

## What the pooled trials found

- A 2023 meta-analysis of **14 trials (573 people)** found pomegranate juice lowered systolic blood
  pressure by about **5 mmHg** on average, and the effect was **dose-dependent** — more juice, more
  effect ([Ghaemi et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37461211/)).
- A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs reached the same conclusion for both systolic
  and diastolic pressure, while noting that the individual trials themselves were inconsistent — which
  is exactly why pooling them matters ([Bahari et al., 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38410857/)).
- The largest and most recent, a 2025 **dose-response meta-analysis of 53 RCTs (2,306 participants)**,
  found pomegranate supplementation reduced both systolic and diastolic blood pressure as part of a
  broader improvement in cardiometabolic risk factors
  ([Mohammadi et al., 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40617711/)).

## The honest caveats

- The effect is **modest** — on the order of a few mmHg, similar to other food-based approaches. It's
  a helpful habit alongside the basics (sodium, weight, activity, medication), not a replacement for them.
- **Dose matters.** The trials that moved the needle generally used a meaningful daily amount of juice
  (around 300 mL). A splash in a smoothie isn't the same intervention.
- **Juice is sugar and calories.** The trials mostly used pomegranate juice; if you're watching blood
  sugar or weight, that's a real trade-off, and whole arils are a lower-sugar way to get the polyphenols.
- Individual trials disagree, so treat this as a gentle, supportive effect — and talk to a clinician
  about managing blood pressure.

The recent evidence, pooled, points the same way: regular pomegranate can nudge blood pressure down a
